**3. Tiếng Anh**

- International students applying for English‑taught master’s programmes at the University of Tübingen must demonstrate C1 level English (CEFR). This can be proved by a bachelor’s degree in English or American Studies equivalent to the Tübingen B.A., or by one of the following certificates: TOEFL iBT ≥ 105 points, IELTS ≥ 7.5, or a Cambridge C1‑level exam (CAE/CPE). A two‑year or longer degree (or HZB) earned in an English‑speaking country (Australia, Canada, Ireland, New Zealand, the United Kingdom or the United States) with official proof that the language of instruction was English also satisfies the requirement and waives the need for a separate test. The university does not list PTE Academic or Duolingo English Test as accepted alternatives.

## **C. XẾP HẠNG, STUDENT VISA, VIỆC LÀM VÀ ĐỜI SỐNG**

**1. Xếp hạng và kiểm định**

- The University of Eberhard Karls Tübingen enrolls roughly 28 000 students, of which about 4 000‑4 500 are international, representing around 15‑16 % of the student body; in the QS World University Rankings it was positioned at 134th globally in 2013 and regularly appears within the top 200, with twelve subjects in the world’s top 200 and several disciplines—including history, linguistics, modern languages, philosophy, earth sciences, life sciences and medicine—ranked inside the top 100, while the Times Higher Education World University Rankings 2025 placed Tübingen 8th among German universities; the university’s strong subject‑specific performance is reflected in its high standings in humanities, life sciences and medicine, and it holds full institutional accreditation from the State of Baden‑Württemberg and is recognised by the German Accreditation Council and AQAS, ensuring that its degrees meet national and EU quality standards.

**2. Student visa và chính sách sau tốt nghiệp**

- Visa/student visa: International students admitted to the University of Tübingen must demonstrate sufficient financial resources for their stay, most commonly by opening a blocked account (Sperrkonto) with at least €992 per month, which equals €11 904 for a full 12‑month period, and for shorter stays the amount is prorated (e.g., five months requires €4 960). The blocked account funds are released in monthly installments after arrival and the account can be opened through any provider approved by German authorities, such as those listed by the DAAD. In addition to a blocked account, a formally notarised letter of commitment (Verpflichtungserklärung) from a German sponsor or a scholarship covering the required amount are also accepted proofs of financing. While studying, non‑EU/EEA students are permitted to work up to 120 full days or 240 half‑days per year, typically limited to 20 hours per week during the semester and full‑time during official holidays, and the income earned may be used to supplement living costs but does not replace the mandatory blocked‑account requirement for the visa and residence permit. EU/EEA nationals and Swiss citizens only need a valid identity document and are not required to provide proof of financial resources for a student visa. Sau tốt nghiệp: The German post‑study “job‑seeker” residence permit, available to non‑EU graduates of any German university—including the University of Tübingen—allows you to stay in Germany for a maximum of 18 months after you have received official proof of graduation (diploma or confirmation of successful completion) and to look for a job that corresponds to your degree; during this period you may also take any employment (full‑time or part‑time) to support yourself, but to convert the permit into a work visa or EU Blue Card you must secure a position related to your field of study. Eligibility requires that you hold a recognised German higher‑education degree (Bachelor, Master or PhD), present a valid passport, prove sufficient financial resources (e.g., a blocked account with at least €1 091 per month, a scholarship, or a formal declaration of commitment), have health‑insurance coverage for the full period, and be registered at a German address (Meldebescheinigung). The application should be submitted to the local Ausländerbehörde as soon as you have the graduation proof—ideally within a few weeks of completing your studies—to avoid delays, and the fee is around €100 with a processing time of 4‑6 weeks.

**3. Cơ hội việc làm, lương và thị trường**

- Cơ hội việc làm: The University of Tübingen provides extensive support for international students seeking internships, Werk‑student positions, and other work‑integrated learning opportunities through its Career Service, which runs a dedicated Praxisportal job and internship board, offers one‑on‑one advising, webinars, workshops, and a DAAD‑funded “Come to Stay” project that helps graduates enter the German job market; while the university does not publish a single unified graduate‑employment figure, recent reports from the university’s own alumni surveys and German higher‑education statistics show that roughly nine out of ten Tübingen graduates find a relevant job or start a doctoral programme within six months of completing their degree, with many securing positions in research institutes, multinational corporations, and the public sector, and a large share of international students report obtaining Werk‑student or internship placements in fields such as life sciences, engineering, computer science, humanities, and economics that often lead to full‑time employment after graduation. Lương & thị trường: In Germany recent university graduates earn on average around €45,000 gross per year, with STEM graduates—particularly engineers and IT specialists—commanding higher starting salaries of €50,000‑€55,000, while graduates in law, economics and social sciences start nearer €40,000‑€44,000; labour‑market data for the Regierungsbezirk Tübingen show a 63.9 % employment rate in 2023, well above the national average, and an unemployment rate of 2.1 %, reflecting strong demand for qualified workers, especially in occupations with recognised shortages such as mechanical, electrical and automotive engineering, software development, data science and health‑care professions like nursing and medical technology; international graduates are highly sought after in these fields, with technical competence outweighing German‑language proficiency and master’s‑level engineers securing jobs more quickly, and given that about 13 % of all students in Germany—roughly 380 000 in the 2023/24 winter term—are international and tend to study engineering, law, economics and social sciences, they align closely with the sectors experiencing the greatest skill gaps, making early career preparation, university‑industry networks and regional support structures crucial for successful integration.
