### **I. Thông tin chung**

**1. Kỳ nhập học, deadline và quy trình apply**

- Leiden University admits international students for two main intakes — the September (fall) intake and the February (spring) intake — and the admission deadlines differ depending on whether a visa or residence permit is required: for the September intake applicants who need a visa or residence permit must apply for admission by 1 April, while those who do not need a visa must submit their application by 15 May; for the February intake the corresponding deadlines are 15 October for visa‑requiring candidates and 15 November for those without visa needs, with all deadlines expiring at 23:59 CET on the stated dates. Prospective students first register their personal details and programme choice in the Dutch national enrollment system Studielink, after which they receive a nomination for a study permit (if applicable) and an invitation to complete Leiden’s online application; the online portal is then used to upload required documents, pay the application fee (which is € 96 for non‑EU/EEA applicants and waived for EU/EEA citizens) and finalize the submission, after which the university reviews the file and issues an admission decision.

**2. Điều kiện đầu vào**

- International students who wish to enroll in a bachelor’s programme at Leiden University must hold a secondary‑school qualification that is recognised as equivalent to the Dutch pre‑university (VWO) diploma – such as the International Baccalaureate, the European Baccalaureate, Cambridge/A‑Level, Pearson Edexcel, OxfordAQA, or a US high‑school diploma with comparable grades – and they must demonstrate sufficient proficiency in English (a VWO final English average of 7.5 or higher, or an equivalent score on an accepted English‑language test). For most programmes the overall grade point average on the VWO‑equivalent diploma must be at least 6.5 to be considered, while selective programmes such as Liberal Arts & Sciences: Global Challenges require a higher average of 7.5 with no individual subject grade below 7 (and a mathematics A grade of 7 or higher). If a candidate’s diploma is not automatically recognised, Leiden may require a VWO‑level entrance examination in the relevant profile subjects (e.g., Mathematics C, English, and the specific subject(s) required for the chosen programme). No portfolio or interview is normally part of the admission process for standard bachelor programmes, although a few highly selective programmes may request additional material; otherwise admission is based solely on the diploma, GPA, subject requirements, and English proficiency.

**3. Tiếng Anh**

- Leiden University requires proof of English‑language proficiency for most master’s and bachelor’s programmes, typically an IELTS Academic score of 6.5 overall with at least 6.0 in each component, a TOEFL iBT score of 90 overall with at least 4.0 (new 1‑6 scale) or 20 (old 0‑30 scale) in each component, or the equivalent Cambridge English C1 Advanced or C2 Proficiency results; it does not accept IELTS Academic online, IELTS General Training, IELTS Indicator, IELTS One‑Skill Retake, TOEFL MyBest, TOEFL Essentials, institutional tests such as ITP, or the TOEFL iBT Home Edition except when test‑centres are inaccessible for safety reasons. Leiden does not list PTE Academic or Duolingo English Test as accepted qualifications. Applicants are exempt from providing an English test result if they have completed a full programme taught in English in the USA, UK, Ireland, New Zealand, Australia, Canada (except French‑taught programmes), Singapore, South Africa or Malta, have an English‑taught International Baccalaureate diploma, hold a Dutch VWO diploma, have earned an English‑taught degree at a Dutch research university, or (for September intake) have completed an English‑taught higher professional education (hbo) programme at a Dutch university of applied sciences.

- International students at Leiden University typically spend between €13 500 and €19 800 per year on living costs, which breaks down roughly as follows: rent (including water, gas and electricity) costs €400–€800 per month (€4 800–€9 600 annually); groceries amount to about €244 per month (€2 928 per year); clothing and shoes average €60 per month (€720 per year); travel expenses range from €70 to €100 per month (€840–€1 200 per year); health insurance is about €133 per month (€1 596 per year); books and study materials cost roughly €49 per month (€588 per year); sport and social activities add €239 per month (€2 868 per year); and telephone expenses are around €19 per month (€228 per year). Adding these items gives a total annual living cost of approximately €13 500 at the low end and €19 800 at the high end, not including tuition fees.

## **C. XẾP HẠNG, STUDENT VISA, VIỆC LÀM VÀ ĐỜI SỐNG**

**1. Xếp hạng và kiểm định**

- Leiden University, the oldest university in the Netherlands, is fully accredited by the Dutch accreditation body NVAO and consistently appears among the world’s top universities, holding the 77th position in the latest Times Higher Education World University Rankings and ranking 26th globally in the 2017 QS World University Rankings; in QS subject rankings its Political Science and International Studies programme was ranked number 1 in the Netherlands and sixth in Europe in 2017, while its Archaeology programme is placed within the global top 10 for the discipline in the most recent QS subject tables, reflecting the university’s strong academic reputation, research impact and appeal to international students.

**2. Student visa (MVV/VVR) và chính sách sau tốt nghiệp**

- Visa/MVV: International students admitted to Leiden University who are non‑EU/EEA nationals must obtain an MVV (machtiging tot voorlopig verblijf) entry visa and a VVR (verblijfsvergunning regulier) residence permit; the university submits the combined TEV application to the Dutch Immigration and Naturalisation Service (IND) on the student’s behalf, and the IND requires proof that the student can cover at least €13 000 per year (approximately €1 083 per month) in living expenses, which can be demonstrated with a blocked Dutch bank account, a scholarship award letter, or a guarantor’s statement that meets IND standards, and the student must also have valid health insurance and an unconditional admission letter; after arrival the student must register at the local municipality within one month, open a Dutch bank account, and ensure the residence card is collected, while any extensions must be filed by the university’s recognized sponsor through the IND portal, and detailed guidance on these requirements can be found on the official IND website, the Nuffic sponsor portal, and studyinnl.org. Sau tốt nghiệp: The Netherlands Orientation Year visa (Zoekjaar voor Hoogopgeleiden) grants recent graduates a one‑year residence permit that allows them to live, work, start a business or undertake an internship in the Netherlands without a separate work permit, and it cannot be extended beyond the 12 months; an international graduate of Leiden University is automatically eligible because Leiden’s bachelor, master or PhD programmes are accredited by the NVAO, and the applicant must apply within three years of receiving the degree, must not have previously held a Zoekjaar permit, and must demonstrate sufficient financial means (roughly €1,200‑€1,500 per month) through bank statements or a sponsor declaration, after which they receive a BSN and can start working even while the IND decision (normally within 90 days) is pending, after which they may later switch to a Highly Skilled Migrant, EU Blue Card or other residence permit if they secure suitable employment.

**3. Cơ hội việc làm, lương và thị trường**

- Cơ hội việc làm: At Leiden University international graduates benefit strongly from internships—both abroad and in the Netherlands—which are consistently cited as the single most valuable experience for launching their careers, and these placements translate into rapid employment: roughly 55 % of International Studies graduates and 50 % of Urban Studies graduates secure a job within two months of graduation, rising to 84 % and 75 % respectively within six months, with about 72 % of International Studies alumni entering a higher‑level professional or academic role; although international alumni face slightly longer job‑search times, higher rates of temporary or on‑call contracts (23 % vs 4 % for Dutch peers) and a higher involuntary unemployment rate (68 % vs 49 %), the majority do obtain stable work—30 % end up in the Netherlands, 37 % in their country of origin (often Germany) and 32 % elsewhere—primarily in semi‑governmental institutions (35 %), financial organisations (13 %), advisory and consultancy (9 %) and non‑profit or humanitarian sectors (7 %). Lương & thị trường: International graduates from Leiden University earn lower average salaries than their Dutch counterparts and are far more likely to hold temporary or on‑call contracts (23 % versus 4 % for Dutch alumni) while being less often in permanent positions (67 % versus 90 %). They are employed more frequently in the profit sector (39 % versus 31 %) and have a slightly higher share of self‑employment (4 % versus 2 %). After graduation they take longer to secure a job, with 68 % reporting involuntary unemployment compared with 49 % of Dutch graduates, and they are more often hired for temporary or project‑based roles rather than stable positions. Although their starting salaries are lower, a larger proportion of international alumni reach earnings above €65 000 per year within five years of graduation, indicating that demand for their skills grows over time, especially in higher‑paid, profit‑oriented occupations.

**4. Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế và đời sống**

- Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế: Leiden University’s International Office, housed within the Student and Educational Affairs (SEA) centre at P.J. Veth Nonnensteeg 1‑3 in Leiden, offers a comprehensive support package for international students that includes a pre‑arrival and on‑campus orientation programme, assistance with finding and securing university‑approved student housing, and a full suite of career services; the orientation covers administrative matters such as admission and enrollment, study‑guidance, personal‑development resources and introductions to Dutch academic life, while the housing support team helps students navigate the competitive Dutch rental market and access university‑managed accommodation, and the Career Services team—located in the Plexus Student Centre in Leiden and also available at the The Hague campus—provides career‑related advice, internship counselling, CV and motivation‑letter reviews, career tests, workshops, information meetings and access to the online LU Career Zone, which together help students transition from study to work and explore post‑graduation opportunities. Đời sống & môi trường: Leiden University’s two campuses in Leiden and The Hague host a vibrant international community where students can live in historic student houses, join a wide range of study and interest associations, and benefit from comprehensive safety services; the study association Custodia, for example, organizes debates, lectures, excursions and social drinks for security‑related programmes, while numerous other clubs cater to diverse cultural and hobby interests, and a dedicated Vietnamese Student Association in Leiden regularly arranges Tet celebrations, language meet‑ups and mentorship for Vietnamese newcomers, providing a supportive network that complements the university’s security infrastructure of on‑site staff, LU‑Card access control, 24‑hour mobile surveillance and a campus‑wide monitoring team that responds to incidents and helps maintain a safe, open environment for all students.
