**3. Tiếng Anh / Tiếng Nhật**

- The University of Tokyo expects all international graduate applicants to prove English proficiency, typically requiring at least an IELTS overall band of 5.5 or a TOEFL iBT score of 72 (with comparable thresholds for other tests such as a PTE Academic score around 58, although the official list emphasizes IELTS and TOEFL), while Duolingo scores are not accepted; however, many graduate schools—including the School of Engineering—state that there is no fixed minimum and evaluate applicants holistically, recommending sufficient English for research and thesis work, and they may waive the English‑language test if the candidate can demonstrate advanced Japanese ability, generally proven by passing the JLPT N1 (or sometimes N2) or an equivalent high‑level Japanese proficiency credential, which is also a prerequisite for enrollment in undergraduate programs taught in Japanese.

**4. Học phí**

- For international students at The University of Tokyo the admission (registration) fee is ¥282,000 and the annual tuition (tuition fee per year) depends on the program: undergraduate programmes require ¥642,960 per year, graduate programmes (excluding the School of Law) require ¥535,800 per year (the same amount for students who enrolled on or before 2024), and the School of Law graduate programmes require ¥520,800 per year; research students pay ¥28,900 per month (¥346,800 per year) and auditing students are charged ¥14,800 per credit. Adding the admission fee, the first‑year total cost is therefore roughly ¥925,000 for undergraduates (¥282,000 + ¥642,960) and ¥858,600 for most graduate students (¥282,000 + ¥535,800). In addition to these core fees, students must also pay examination fees – for example ¥4,000 (first stage) for undergraduates, ¥30,000 (first stage) and ¥13,000‑23,000 (second stage) for most graduate programmes, and ¥7,000 (first stage) for the School of Law – and any other programme‑specific charges; all amounts are quoted in Japanese yen (JPY).

**5. Chi phí sinh hoạt**

- For international students at the University of Tokyo the typical annual living cost is roughly ¥1.5 million, broken down as follows: accommodation in university‑affiliated dorms or private one‑room apartments averages about ¥80,000 per month (≈¥960,000 per year); food expenses are about ¥30,000 per month (≈¥360,000 per year); local transport (subway, bus, occasional taxis) runs around ¥10,000 per month (≈¥120,000 per year); and the mandatory National Health Insurance premium is roughly ¥20,000–¥25,000 per year, giving a total of approximately ¥1,460,000–¥1,470,000 annually.

**Yêu cầu học thuật chi tiết**

- The University of Tokyo requires all undergraduates—including international students—to complete a two‑year Junior Division on the Komaba Campus, where they enroll in one of six streams (Humanities and Social Sciences I–III or Natural Sciences I–III) and follow a curriculum organized into Foundation, Intermediate, Integrated and Thematic courses that together total the credit units needed for progression to the Senior Division; courses are assigned specific credit values—most Global Unit summer courses carry 2 credits, while shorter modules such as a 5‑session online class count as 0.5 credit—and many are open without prior prerequisites, though some advanced seminars expect completion of earlier foundational classes; the program’s learning outcomes emphasize a liberal‑arts education that develops broad cultural sensitivity, critical thinking, problem‑solving, experimental and design skills, and the ability to conduct research, as reflected in mandatory First‑Year Seminars that teach academic information‑retrieval, scholarly writing, teamwork, multimedia production and active participation in academic debates, preparing students for specialized senior‑division study and potential credit transfer to home institutions.

## **C. XẾP HẠNG, STUDENT VISA, VIỆC LÀM VÀ ĐỜI SỐNG**

**1. Xếp hạng và kiểm định**

- The University of Tokyo enrolls a total of 4,851 international students (285 + 113 undergraduates, 1,757 + 728 master’s students, 141 + 74 professional students, 2,167 + 787 PhD students and 501 + 216 research students and auditors), and is officially recognized by Japan’s Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology (MEXT) while its engineering programmes hold accreditation from the Japan Accreditation Board for Engineering Education (JABEE); in the 2024 QS World University Rankings the university is placed 22nd globally, in the 2026 Times Higher Education World University Rankings it ranks 26th, and its leading subject positions include QS rankings of 12th for Natural Sciences, 15th for Engineering & Technology, 21st for Life Sciences & Medicine, 20th for Social Sciences and 28th for Arts & Humanities.

**2. Student visa (COE) và chính sách sau tốt nghiệp**

- Visa/COE: International students admitted to The University of Tokyo must first obtain a Certificate of Eligibility (COE) from the university’s International Student Section, submitting a recent passport‑size photo, a copy of the passport, and documents proving financial support—either a scholarship certificate, a bank balance statement in the student’s name, or an Expenditure Defrayal Certificate from a guarantor—along with any required forms; once the COE is issued (typically within three months), it is presented at a Japanese embassy or consulate to obtain a “College Student” visa, which requires proof that the applicant can cover tuition and living costs for the full study period (generally around ¥1.5 million per year, though exact figures are listed on the Ministry of Foreign Affairs’ Study in Japan portal at studyinjapan.go.jp); after arrival, visa holders may engage in part‑time work up to 28 hours per week (or up to 40 hours during school vacations) provided they obtain a “Permission to Engage in Activity Other Than That Permitted” from the Immigration Services Agency, ensuring compliance with the official regulations posted on MOFA’s website. Sau tốt nghiệp: International graduates of the University of Tokyo who have completed a bachelor’s, master’s or doctoral programme may change their residence status from “Student” to “Designated Activities (Continuing Job Hunting)” and remain in Japan to look for employment. Applicants must apply within one year of graduation, be at least 18 years old, and be able to show roughly ¥200,000 in personal savings to support themselves while job‑searching. The initial period of stay is six months, and a single six‑month extension is normally granted, allowing a maximum of one year of designated‑activities status for job hunting. The visa does not permit other remunerative work beyond the job‑search activities.

**3. Cơ hội việc làm, lương và thị trường**

- Cơ hội việc làm: The University of Tokyo provides extensive career‑support services tailored to international students, including year‑round seminars on job‑hunting and internship strategies, individual counseling on resume writing, interview techniques, and visa‑related issues, as well as large‑scale job‑fair events with company booths and online workshops that connect students with Japanese and multinational firms for internships, co‑op placements, and full‑time recruitment; as a result, the university reports a very high graduate‑employment rate for its international student cohort—typically around 90‑95 % of graduates secure employment or continued study within six months of graduation, with many entering fields such as engineering, science, business, and public‑policy roles. Lương & thị trường: In Japan the average starting salary for university graduates in 2023 is about ¥3.5 million annually, rising to ¥4.5‑5 million for engineering and IT majors, and international graduates earn roughly comparable wages but only about 31 % of the 51,600 graduates who arrived in 2017 remained employed, with the largest numbers coming from China, Vietnam, Nepal and Korea and most concentrated in Tokyo (over 10,000), Osaka, Saitama and Kanagawa, while the labour market shows acute shortages especially in information‑technology fields—an estimated 220,000 vacant IT positions by 2025‑26 with strong demand for AI, cybersecurity, cloud‑computing and data‑science skills—plus persistent gaps in nursing, construction and advanced manufacturing, driving firms to hire both domestic and foreign graduates with these competencies.

**4. Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế và đời sống**

- Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế: The University of Tokyo’s International Student Support Room (ISSR) within the Center for Global Education provides comprehensive services for international students, including a structured orientation program that introduces new arrivals to visa procedures, campus facilities, safety, daily life and academic expectations; housing assistance that offers detailed information on university residence halls, private apartments, the guarantor system, moving‑in/out procedures and nearby accommodation options; career support that features individual counseling, job‑hunting seminars, internship listings, guidance on work visas and residence status, as well as a newsletter of on‑ and off‑campus opportunities; and academic tutoring and faculty‑staff consultations arranged through ISSR to help students with coursework and adapt to university studies. Đời sống & môi trường: The University of Tokyo offers a comprehensive support system for international students that includes orientation programs, housing assistance, daily‑life guidance, and a dedicated International Student Support Room that provides counseling, safety and disaster‑prevention information, and career advice; on both the Komaba and Kashiwa campuses students can join a wide variety of student clubs and “circles” ranging from cultural and language groups to sports and hobby societies, which are promoted through the Center for Global Education’s Interaction Activities portal, and there are specific Vietnamese student communities and associations that organize social events, language exchange sessions, and peer‑mentoring activities to help Vietnamese newcomers connect with fellow compatriots and other international peers while benefiting from Japan’s overall reputation for safety, efficient public transportation, and a campus environment that emphasizes emergency preparedness and inclusive campus life.
