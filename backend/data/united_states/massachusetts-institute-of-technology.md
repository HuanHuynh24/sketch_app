**2. Điều kiện đầu vào (GPA, SAT/ACT)**

- MIT’s undergraduate admission for international students looks for a consistently exceptional academic record—typically a GPA in the top 10 percent of a student’s class (often 3.9 or higher on an unweighted 4.0 scale) and strong grades in core math and science courses such as calculus, physics, chemistry and biology. Applicants must submit official secondary‑school transcripts (học bạ) and, for the 2024‑2025 cycle onward, provide either SAT scores (evidence of ≥ 1500 total, with ≥ 740 Math and ≥ 730 EBRW) or ACT scores (composite ≥ 34, with ≥ 34 in Math and English). While SAT/ACT are required, MIT does not mandate SAT Subject Tests, AP, or IB exams, though high scores in AP/IB math, physics, chemistry, or computer science are viewed favorably and can strengthen the application. English proficiency must be demonstrated by TOEFL iBT (≥ 100) or IELTS (≥ 7.5) for non‑native speakers. No formal portfolio is required except for a few specialized programs (e.g., Architecture) that may request work samples, and the admissions interview is optional, conducted by a volunteer alumnus rather than as a formal evaluation. **3. Tiếng Anh**

- International graduate applicants to MIT who are not native English speakers must submit an English‑language proficiency test, with the accepted exams being the TOEFL (minimum 90 iBT, 233 computer‑based or 577 paper‑based) or the IELTS (minimum overall band 7.0); other tests such as Duolingo English Test or PTE are not listed as acceptable by MIT. Scores are valid for two years from the test date. Applicants may be granted a waiver of the TOEFL/IELTS requirement if they (a) received primary and secondary education in English, (b) have lived in an English‑speaking country for four years or more, (c) earned their first degree from an institution in the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia or New Zealand, or (d) earned a first degree from Singapore’s NUS or NTU; such waivers are considered case‑by‑case and must be requested in writing with the application.

**2. Student Visa (F-1) và Chính sách OPT**

- Student Visa (F-1): To obtain an F‑1 student visa for MIT, an international student must present a valid passport, an approved F‑1 visa stamp (except Canadian or Bermudian citizens), the most recent MIT Form I‑20, proof of payment of the I‑901 SEVIS fee, the MIT admission letter, and documented proof of financial support that meets the amount shown on the I‑20; this financial proof can include recent bank statements, scholarship or fellowship award letters, or a sponsor’s affidavit that together cover tuition, fees, and living expenses for at least one academic year, and it must be signed and dated by the sponsor or financial institution, with any large deposits explained, as required by U.S. Department of State and USCIS guidelines for F‑1 visa applicants. Sau tốt nghiệp (OPT/STEM OPT): International students graduating from MIT on an F‑1 visa may apply for the 24‑month STEM Optional Practical Training (STEM‑OPT) extension after completing their regular post‑completion OPT. To be eligible, the student must hold a bachelor’s, master’s or doctorate degree that appears on the U.S. Department of Homeland Security’s STEM‑Designated Degree Program List, must be in an active period of post‑completion OPT at the time of filing, and must have a job offer from an employer who is enrolled in and using the E‑Verify system. The application (Form I‑765) must be submitted to USCIS within 60 days of the OPT EAD expiration and no later than the end of the current OPT period; while the STEM‑OPT petition is pending, the student may continue to work for up to 180 days beyond the original OPT end date, but not beyond the date USCIS makes a final decision. The extension provides up to an additional 24 months of work authorization, during which the student must report employment changes to their school’s DSO and comply with all reporting requirements.

**4. Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế và đời sống**

- Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế: MIT’s International Students Office (ISO) serves as the primary hub for all international student needs, offering a comprehensive orientation program that includes pre‑arrival webinars, arrival‑week sessions, and ongoing workshops to help new students adjust to life in the United States; the office also provides housing support by advising on on‑campus options, connecting students with the MIT Housing Office and off‑campus resources, and offering guidance on lease negotiations and roommate matching; in addition, ISO works closely with MIT’s Career Development Center and the Global Education Office to deliver career services such as résumé reviews, interview practice, internship and job search assistance, networking events, and counseling on global work‑study and study‑abroad opportunities, ensuring that international students have coordinated access to both academic and professional development resources throughout their time at MIT. Đời sống & môi trường: MIT enrolls a large and diverse international population—roughly one‑third of undergraduates and about half of graduate students come from outside the United States—who benefit from a vibrant campus life that includes more than 450 student‑run clubs covering everything from robotics and clean‑energy competitions to dance, art, entrepreneurship and cultural organizations, as well as a strong Greek presence with numerous fraternities and sororities that offer social, service and leadership opportunities while being overseen by campus safety programs such as the MIT Police, 24‑hour shuttles, SafeRide and comprehensive risk‑management policies that address housing and event security; for Vietnamese students, MIT hosts an active Vietnamese Student Association and related cultural clubs that organize language‑exchange events, celebrations of Tết, community service projects and networking gatherings, providing a supportive community within the broader international student ecosystem.
