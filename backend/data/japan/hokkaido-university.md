**3. Tiếng Anh / Tiếng Nhật**

- Hokkaido University requires international applicants whose first language is not Japanese to demonstrate English proficiency, accepting TOEFL iBT (including Home Edition) with a minimum overall score of 80, IELTS Academic with an overall band of 6.0 (no individual band below 5.5), TOEIC Listening & Reading with a minimum of 730, the Test in Practical English Proficiency (EIKEN) Grade 1, Cambridge English exams such as CAE or CPE, and the United Nations Associations Test of English (UNA TE); while PTE Academic is treated as equivalent to TOEFL/IELTS and is accepted if a score of roughly 50 or higher is submitted, Duolingo English Test is not listed among the accepted exams. Applicants who have earned Japanese‑Language Proficiency Test (JLPT) N1 or a score of 270 or higher on the Japanese‑as‑a‑Foreign‑Language section of the Examination for Japanese University Admission for International Students (EJU) may be exempt from the English requirement when the program is taught in Japanese or when the university determines that sufficient Japanese proficiency demonstrates the ability to study without additional English proof.

**4. Học phí**

- Hokkaido University charges international students tuition of ¥267,900 each semester, amounting to ¥535,800 for the full academic year; the mandatory enrollment (admission) fee is ¥282,000, and the online application process adds an administrative charge of ¥500 together with an examination fee (generally around ¥30,000), so the baseline total cost for a first‑year international student is approximately ¥818,300 plus the exam fee, all amounts being expressed in Japanese yen (JPY).

- International students admitted to Hokkaido University must have completed a twelve‑year secondary education equivalent to a Japanese high‑school graduate and demonstrate sufficient language proficiency—typically JLPT N2 (or higher) for Japanese‑medium tracks or TOEFL/IELTS scores for English‑medium programs—along with any required standardized test results specified in the admission guidelines. **2. Student visa (COE) và chính sách sau tốt nghiệp**

- Visa/COE: International students admitted to Hokkaido University must first obtain a Certificate of Eligibility (COE) issued by the university’s International Office before applying for a student visa at a Japanese embassy or consulate, and the COE must cover a stay of more than three months; once the visa is stamped, students are permitted to work up to 28 hours per week (maximum 8 hours per day during school breaks) provided they obtain a “Permission to Engage in Remunerative Activities” (part‑time‑job certificate) from the Immigration Bureau, which can be applied for either on entry if the COE was issued in advance or later by submitting the required form in black ink; the Ministry of Foreign Affairs requires proof of sufficient funds to cover tuition and living expenses for the entire program (commonly demonstrated by a bank balance of roughly ¥1 million per year or a scholarship guarantee), and while the university does not fund regular tuition, it offers emergency financial assistance such as an interest‑free lump‑sum loan up to ¥50 000 and the Michiko Seki International Student Support Fund for students who encounter unexpected hardships, both of which require an application through the academic affairs office and are limited to those not already receiving government scholarships. Sau tốt nghiệp: International graduates of Hokkaido University who have completed a regular full‑time undergraduate or graduate program and are currently residing in Japan on a student visa may change their status of residence to “Designated Activities (Job Hunting)” (Immigration Services Agency category No. 46) to remain in Japan for up to six months to look for employment, with the possibility of one renewal for an additional six months, allowing a maximum total stay of one year; eligibility requires graduation from a full‑time degree program (exchange, research or other non‑degree students are excluded), possession of a valid passport, proof of sufficient funds, and a Certificate of Eligibility issued for more than three months, and the application—detailed on the official ISA site studyinjapan.go.jp—must be submitted to the regional Immigration Services Bureau together with the required documents such as the graduation certificate, residence card, and a written statement of job‑search activities, after which part‑time work up to 28 hours per week is permitted while seeking a full‑time position.

**3. Cơ hội việc làm, lương và thị trường**

- Cơ hội việc làm: Hokkaido University provides extensive career‑support services for its international student body, including a dedicated Career Center that offers résumé critique, mock‑interview practice, job‑hunting seminars, company‑research events, and direct assistance with securing internships and co‑op placements; the International Student Center also runs Business‑Japanese classes and collaborates with the Career Center to deliver workshops and employment‑related postings, while a specialized “Career Support Network” supplies additional internship listings and guidance for transitioning from a student visa to a work visa, and the university’s overall graduate‑employment rate remains high—exceeding 95 % of students (including international graduates) securing employment or further study within one year of graduation—reflecting strong outcomes in a wide range of sectors such as government, industry, research institutes, and NGOs. Lương & thị trường: Graduate salaries in Japan remain around ¥228,000–¥230,000 per month for new university graduates, with engineers and researchers commanding modest premiums, while doctoral holders in engineering and university‑faculty positions earn substantially more than master’s graduates; the labour market continues to show strong demand for engineers, researchers, physicians and university faculty, yet regional disparities persist, as Hokkaido (including the Hokkaido & Tohoku area) records a lower graduate employment rate of roughly 83 % compared with the national 92 % rate, reflecting weaker local demand for international graduates who are most concentrated in social‑science and engineering fields.
