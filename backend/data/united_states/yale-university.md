### **I. Thông tin chung**

**1. Kỳ nhập học, deadline và quy trình apply**

- Yale University admits undergraduate international students only for the fall semester, with no spring entry option, and offers a single‑choice early action (restrictive early action) deadline of November 1 and a regular‑decision deadline of January 2; applicants must submit either the Common Application or the Coalition Application together with Yale’s $80 application fee (or a fee‑waiver request), the required supplemental essays, transcripts, and any standardized‑test scores, and for early‑action candidates the financial‑aid form is due by November 10 while regular‑decision applicants must file it by February 25, after which admission decisions are released in mid‑December for early action and by early April for regular decision, after which accepted students receive visa‑processing guidance from Yale’s Office of International Students and Scholars.

**2. Điều kiện đầu vào (GPA, SAT/ACT)**

- Yale University’s undergraduate admission for international applicants requires a completed Common Application (or Coalition Application) plus the Yale‑specific supplement, an official high‑school transcript (học bạ) translated into English, and proof of English proficiency—typically a TOEFL iBT score of 100 + (with ≥28 on speaking and listening) or an IELTS band of 7 +; these language tests are waived if the applicant’s previous degree was earned at a four‑year institution where instruction was in English. While Yale is test‑optional for the 2025–2026 cycle, submitted SAT scores of 1480 + (or ACT composite 33 +) and strong AP or IB results (scores of 5 on AP exams or 6–7 on IB Higher Level) remain advantageous and are used to gauge academic rigor alongside a competitive GPA—most admitted students present an un‑weighted high‑school GPA of 3.5 – 4.0 (or the local equivalent). For schools that demand visual‑arts preparation—such as the School of Art and the School of Architecture—applicants must also submit a portfolio that showcases original work meeting the program’s specific guidelines, and some programs may request an interview (often conducted virtually) to discuss the portfolio and artistic intent; however, for most other Yale colleges a formal interview is not required, though optional alumni or admissions‑staff interviews are offered for additional context.

**3. Tiếng Anh**

- Yale University requires international applicants whose first language or primary language of instruction is not English to submit an English‑proficiency test score; the accepted exams are the TOEFL iBT (minimum overall score 100, with competitive applicants scoring 105 or higher and speaking/listening sub‑scores of at least 28), the IELTS (minimum overall band 7.0, with competitive scores of 8.0 or higher), and the Duolingo English Test (minimum overall score 120, with competitive scores of 130 or higher). A waiver is granted when the applicant has earned a baccalaureate degree (or foreign equivalent) from a four‑year college or university where English was the primary language of instruction and the applicant has studied in residence for at least three years (some programs note 2.5 years of English‑medium study). The School of Public Health explicitly does not accept Duolingo scores, and Yale’s published requirements do not list the PTE as an accepted test.

**Yêu cầu học thuật chi tiết**

- International students at Yale must maintain full‑time status by enrolling in at least two credit‑bearing courses each summer session, typically two 1‑credit courses, or a combination such as a 1.5‑credit science course with a lab plus an additional 1‑credit course to meet the 2‑credit minimum (2.5 credits in that case); for undergraduate programs, Yale College requires all students to complete two credits in quantitative reasoning, two credits in writing, and a foreign‑language requirement fulfilled with one to three courses depending on prior proficiency, alongside distributional requirements of at least two credits each in the humanities and arts, the sciences, and the social sciences, with certain courses (e.g., ECON 121/125 for microeconomics and ECON 122/126 for macroeconomics) providing acceleration credit toward those requirements, thereby structuring the curriculum to ensure students achieve foundational quantitative, communication, language, and disciplinary breadth outcomes.

## **C. XẾP HẠNG, STUDENT VISA, VIỆC LÀM VÀ ĐỜI SỐNG**

**1. Xếp hạng và kiểm định**

- Yale University, a private research institution in New Haven, Connecticut, enrolls roughly 9 percent of its undergraduate body and 16 percent of all students as internationals, with higher shares in graduate programs such as 8.2 percent of MD students and 29 percent of PhD candidates in the sciences; the university is regionally accredited by the New England Commission of Higher Education (NECHE) and its professional schools hold specialized accreditations—including the School of Medicine (LCME), School of Law (ABA), School of Management (AACSB), School of Nursing (CCNE), and School of Public Health (CEPHE)—and it consistently ranks among the world’s elite, placing #5 in the 2024 U.S. News & World Report National Universities list, #14 in the 2024 QS World University Rankings, and #5 in the 2024 Times Higher Education World University Rankings, with subject‑specific strengths that place Yale in the top 10 globally for disciplines such as Law, Education, Clinical, and Social Sciences across both QS and THE subject‑ranking tables.

**2. Student Visa (F-1) và Chính sách OPT**

- Student Visa (F-1): International students admitted to Yale University receive a Form I‑20 (Certificate of Eligibility for Nonimmigrant Student Status) from Yale’s Office of International Students & Scholars after submitting admission paperwork and documented proof that they can cover the full Cost of Attendance for at least the first academic year, which includes tuition and fees, estimated living expenses, and any dependent costs; acceptable evidence typically consists of recent bank statements, scholarship award letters, sponsor letters, or a U.S.‑based loan guarantee that together meet or exceed Yale’s published figures, and the student must also pay the SEVIS I‑901 fee and keep the I‑20 signed by the Designated School Official; for the F‑1 visa application, the student presents the signed I‑20, a valid passport, a completed DS‑160 confirmation page, a recent U.S. visa‑type photograph, the SEVIS fee receipt, and the same financial documentation at the U.S. embassy or consulate, as required by travel.state.gov, and once in the United States must maintain full‑time enrollment, keep the I‑20 up‑to‑date, and comply with USCIS regulations regarding employment, travel, and status changes. Sau tốt nghiệp (OPT/STEM OPT): International graduates from Yale University on an F‑1 visa may extend their post‑completion Optional Practical Training (OPT) by a 24‑month STEM OPT extension if they have earned a bachelor’s, master’s or doctorate degree in a field whose CIP code appears on the Department of Education’s STEM Designated Degree Program List, and the degree was obtained from an accredited, SEVP‑certified U.S. institution. The student must receive a new Form I‑20 with a STEM OPT recommendation from Yale’s Designated School Official, file Form I‑765 (with the required fee, employer’s E‑Verify information and a copy of the STEM degree) no earlier than 90 days before the current post‑completion OPT expires and no later than 60 days after the DSO’s SEVIS entry, and must be employed by an E‑Verify‑registered employer who provides a training plan and compensation comparable to that of similarly situated U.S. workers. Only one STEM OPT extension may be granted per qualifying degree, and the student may not accrue more than 150 days of unemployment across the combined 12‑month OPT and 24‑month STEM period; if the extension application is pending when the original OPT ends, employment authorization automatically continues for up to 180 days while USCIS adjudicates the case.

**3. Cơ hội việc làm, lương và thị trường**

- Cơ hội việc làm (CPT/OPT): International students at Yale who complete Curricular Practical Training (CPT) internships experience very strong placement outcomes, with roughly 84 % receiving a full‑time job offer within three months of graduation and overall full‑time employment rates for the class hovering around 80 % of graduates seeking work; the median weekly salary for international hires is about $2,645 (approximately $137 k annually), and they are represented across the same sectors as U.S. graduates, with about two‑thirds entering for‑profit firms and one‑third joining non‑profits, NGOs, government or other public agencies, and the most common post‑graduation fields include finance/insurance/real‑estate, technology, consulting, healthcare/biotech, and academia/research. Lương & thị trường: International graduates from Yale’s graduate programs earn a median base salary of roughly $127 000, compared with a median of $175 000 for U.S. citizens and permanent residents, while the median weekly earnings for international graduates are about $2 645 (versus $2 570 for U.S. graduates); Yale College graduates entering the broader workforce report a mean starting salary of $94 000. Across the United States, the Bureau of Labor Statistics identifies several high‑demand occupations that align with these earnings, notably consulting (median annual earnings $120 000–$150 000 and strong hiring across both private‑sector firms and government), finance and accounting roles (median $95 000–$130 000 with faster‑than‑average job growth), and information‑technology positions such as network and computer‑systems administration (median $75 000–$100 000 and steady demand). Additional fast‑growing fields include market‑research analysts and marketing specialists, occupational therapists, compensation‑benefits analysts, and genetic counselors, all posting median salaries in the $75 000–$100 000 range and projected employment growth well above the national average, making them attractive targets for Yale international alumni seeking strong labour‑market prospects.

**4. Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế và đời sống**

- Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế: Yale’s Office of International Students and Scholars (OISS) serves more than 8,000 international students, scholars, staff and their families, providing comprehensive immigration advising, employment guidance, cultural‑adjustment resources and a central hub for information on living in New Haven; the office runs a dedicated Orientation for International Students (OIS) program that introduces newcomers to campus services, academic expectations and community life, and it partners with Yale Housing to help students locate on‑campus or off‑campus housing, understand lease options and navigate roommate matching; in addition, OISS collaborates closely with the Office of Career Strategy to offer career counseling, internship and job search workshops, employer networking events and sponsorship guidance tailored to the specific visa and work‑authorization needs of international students, ensuring they receive coordinated support from orientation through housing to post‑graduation career planning. Đời sống & môi trường: Yale University welcomes a large and diverse international student body—over 8,500 students and scholars from more than 145 countries—who benefit from dedicated support through the Office of International Students & Scholars, the MacMillan Center for International & Area Studies, the Yale Office of International Affairs, and peer liaison programs that help with immigration, cultural adjustment, academic advising, and social connections; campus life is vibrant with dozens of cultural, religious, and resource centers as well as a wide array of student organizations, including Greek‑letter fraternities and sororities, and many clubs such as the Vietnamese Student Association that bring together Vietnamese students and others interested in Vietnamese culture, providing social events, mentorship, and community outreach; safety is a top priority, with Yale publishing annual Clery Act reports that detail campus crime statistics, fire safety procedures, and security policies, and the university maintains a well‑staffed police department, emergency alert systems, and resources like Student Accessibility Services to ensure a secure environment for all students.
