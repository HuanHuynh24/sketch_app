### **I. Thông tin chung**

**1. Kỳ nhập học, deadline và quy trình apply**

- International applicants to Freie Universität Berlin can enroll in either the winter (October start) or summer (April start) semester; the university’s official deadlines are 1 April for the fall‑semester intake (which begins in October) and 1 November for the spring‑semester intake (which begins in April), with the receipt of all required documents by those dates being decisive, while many master programmes also list the customary 15 July deadline for winter admission and 15 January for summer admission; the application fee is €75 (paid to uni‑assist for the pre‑evaluation document (VPD) and subsequently to FU‑Berlin when submitting the online portal application), and the procedure for non‑German degree holders is to first create a VPD request on the uni‑assist website, pay the €75 fee and wait four to six weeks for processing, then log into the FU‑Berlin application portal during the open period, upload the VPD together with all certified documents, complete the online form, pay the fee and submit; all documents must be signed where required, and applicants must also provide proof of mandatory health insurance.

**5. Chi phí sinh hoạt**

- International students at the Free University of Berlin pay no tuition, only a semester contribution of about €311 that includes a public‑transport ticket, while accommodation costs range from roughly €3,100 to €4,900 per semester for university‑run housing (student‑village single‑occupancy, homestay or the Social Hub) or can be arranged independently at the student’s own expense; a German insurance package covering health, accident and liability costs €200 per semester and is included in the program fee, food expenses average €200‑€250 per month, and transport is already covered by the semester ticket; for visa purposes a blocked‑account of €1,200 per month (≈ €14,400 per year) is required, but most students budget around €10,000‑€12,000 annually to cover housing, food, transport, insurance and personal costs.

**Yêu cầu học thuật chi tiết**

- At the Freie Universität Berlin international (guest) students enroll in bachelor programmes that follow the European Credit Transfer System, where one ECTS credit represents roughly 30 hours of total student workload and a standard six‑semester “mono” (single‑subject) degree comprises 180 credits (30 credits each semester); the curriculum is organised into a core subject of about 90 credits plus related or “affine” modules that can add up to another 30 credits if language or other prior skills are demonstrated, while the remaining credits are distributed among elective courses and required internships, and students must submit an ECTS Learning Agreement before starting their studies to have their planned courses recognised; most mono programmes list no specific academic prerequisites beyond adequate language proficiency, and the learning outcomes are defined per module – for example, introductory archaeology modules aim to familiarize students with methodological and theoretical approaches, enable independent research planning and presentation, and develop the ability to justify research questions and methods, while other modules target practical experience in international research cooperation and the articulation of scientific positions in front of an international audience.

## **C. XẾP HẠNG, STUDENT VISA, VIỆC LÀM VÀ ĐỜI SỐNG**

**1. Xếp hạng và kiểm định**

- Freie Universität Berlin is a publicly funded research university in Berlin that holds institutional accreditation from the Berlin Senate Department for Education, Youth and Science, is recognised by the Wissenschaftsrat and undergoes programme‑level quality assurance by the German accreditation agency AQAS; in the latest QS World University Rankings (2025) it is placed 97th globally and ranks fourth among German universities, while the Times Higher Education World University Rankings 2024 position it within the top 100 (71st worldwide); in QS subject‑specific rankings 2024 the university is 27th in Life Sciences & Medicine, 34th in Social Sciences & Management, 46th in Arts & Humanities, 71st in Natural Sciences and 103rd in Engineering & Technology, and international students constitute about 18 % of its roughly 32 000 students (≈5 800 international enrolments).

**2. Student visa và chính sách sau tốt nghiệp**

- Visa/student visa: International students admitted to the Free University of Berlin must demonstrate sufficient financial means for a German student visa, typically by opening a blocked account (Sperrkonto) that provides at least €992 per month (the current threshold as of September 2024) for living expenses, or by presenting an official scholarship or a formal obligation letter covering the same amount; once in Germany they may also work up to 120 full‑time days (or 240 half‑days) per year without a work permit, allowing part‑time employment alongside their studies, and the DAAD’s study‑in‑germany.de portal outlines these requirements and confirms that a blocked account is the most widely accepted proof of financial resources for the visa and residence permit process. Sau tốt nghiệp: International graduates from the Free University of Berlin (or any other German higher‑education institution) may remain in Germany for up to 18 months after completing a recognized Bachelor’s, Master’s or PhD programme by applying for a residence permit for job‑seeking under Section 20 (1) No. 1 of the Residence Act; the permit is an entitlement for third‑country nationals who can provide proof of graduation (diploma, transcript or confirmation letter), a valid passport, health‑insurance coverage for the whole period, sufficient financial means (e.g., a blocked‑account statement or a declaration of commitment), a current registration certificate (Meldebescheinigung) and evidence of accommodation, and must be submitted before the existing student residence expires (ideally within the first three months after graduation). During the 18 months the holder may work in any occupation to support themselves, and once a qualified job matching the degree is found they can convert the permit into a skilled‑worker residence permit or an EU Blue Card. The visa cannot be extended beyond the 18‑month maximum.

**3. Cơ hội việc làm, lương và thị trường**

- Cơ hội việc làm: The Freie Universität Berlin’s Career Service dedicates a full‑time programme to international students, offering tailored advice, English‑language internship listings (especially in Berlin startups across marketing, software development, community and visual design, and online marketing), support in finding Werkstudent (working‑student) positions, and access to the Alumniportal Deutschland for post‑graduation job opportunities, while also providing clear guidance on the legal limits for student employment (up to 120 days per year or 240 days with a degree‑related role); as a result, graduates from the university enjoy a very strong labour‑market outcome, with roughly 85‑90 % securing paid employment or commencing a PhD within six months of graduation, many entering research, academia, consulting, engineering, IT, finance, media and related sectors, and the combination of internships and Werkstudent experience is recognised as a key factor that boosts these employment rates. Lương & thị trường: Graduates in Germany can generally expect starting salaries of roughly 45 000 € per year, with higher earnings in technically‑oriented fields – for example, engineering disciplines such as mechanical, electrical or automotive engineering often start around 50 000–55 000 €, while computer‑science and data‑science graduates commonly begin at 55 000 € or more; humanities and social‑science graduates tend to start closer to 38 000 €. The labour market shows a strong demand for occupations that require specialised technical expertise, notably engineers, IT developers, data analysts, and specialists in renewable‑energy technology, as well as healthcare professionals and finance analysts. For international graduates, especially those from the Free University of Berlin, strong technical skills can compensate for limited German language proficiency, though proficiency still improves job prospects; successful entry into the market also depends on early career preparation, networking with regional industry partners and taking advantage of the university’s career‑service programmes that connect students with internships and employer events.

**4. Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế và đời sống**

- Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế: The International Student Support (ISS) at Freie Universität Berlin, launched in September 2023, serves as the central office for international prospective and current students, offering regular information events—both online and in‑person—on study orientation, the German higher‑education system, enrolment formalities, and how to find accommodation, as well as introductory workshops on university culture, academic work and stress‑management; ISS provides general academic advising, psychological counselling, and group counselling sessions after these events and directs specific queries to the university’s Info‑Service Studium, while the university’s Career Service assists international students with internships, job‑search strategies and the German labour market, and the Studierendenwerk Berlin InfoPoint and Social‑Counselling offices (located at Otto‑von‑Simson‑Str. 26 and Thielallee 38) offer housing guidance, accommodation listings and additional social support for FU‑Berlin students. Đời sống & môi trường: The Free University of Berlin offers a vibrant campus life for international students, highlighted by the International Club, a student‑run organization that brings together more than 300 members from Germany and abroad and hosts over 80 events each academic year to promote intercultural dialogue, social activities, and city explorations; the club also serves as a hub for cultural groups such as a Vietnamese Student Association, which regularly arranges gatherings, language‑exchange evenings and cultural celebrations for Vietnamese students and anyone interested in Vietnamese culture, fostering a supportive community within the larger international network; safety on campus is a priority, with a dedicated university police service operating around the clock, well‑signposted emergency phones, a clear code of conduct that prohibits discrimination and violence, and easy access to counseling and assistance through the International House and student support offices, ensuring that students feel secure while studying and living in Berlin.
