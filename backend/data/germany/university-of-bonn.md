**2. Điều kiện đầu vào**

- International applicants to the University of Bonn must hold a university‑entrance qualification that is recognised as equivalent to the German Abitur; for non‑EU/EEA candidates this equivalence is normally verified through uni‑assist, which issues a pre‑assessment (VPD) that converts the original GPA or “học bạ” into the German grading scale, checks that the required subjects (e.g., mathematics and a science for most natural‑science Bachelors, or language and humanities for arts‑related programmes) have been passed, and confirms that all transcripts, certificates and exam results are authentic; admission to German‑taught programmes additionally requires a German language proficiency certificate (usually B2 or C1), while English‑taught master’s programmes require proof of English (e.g., TOEFL/IELTS) and a relevant first degree with a minimum grade (often a 2.5 / ‘good’ GPA or better), and some specialised master’s (such as design, architecture or fine arts) may also ask for a portfolio and/or a short interview, whereas most other master’s and all bachelor programmes admit students solely on the basis of the VPD‑validated academic record and language proof.

**3. Tiếng Anh**

- International students applying to English‑taught programmes at the University of Bonn must demonstrate a B2 level of English (CEFR) by submitting one of the recognised certificates: TOEFL (any score meeting the B2 threshold), IELTS (any band meeting the B2 threshold), Cambridge English B2 First, TELC English B2, PTE Academic with a minimum score of 59, or UNICERT II Vantage; Duolingo English Test is not listed among the accepted tests. An exemption from submitting an English certificate is granted in specific cases, typically when the applicant’s previous degree was taught entirely in English or when other university‑approved evidence of sufficient English proficiency is provided.

**4. Học phí**

- The University of Bonn does not charge tuition fees to international students, so the annual tuition (học phí) is €0; instead, students pay a mandatory semester contribution (social fee) of about €300 each semester, amounting to roughly €600 per academic year (tổng học phí / semester contribution), and there is no separate application fee (phí đăng ký) as applications to degree programmes are free, with no other mandatory charges beyond the semester contribution.

**5. Chi phí sinh hoạt**

- International students at the University of Bonn should budget roughly €1,000 – €1,200 per month for living costs, which breaks down to €250 – €560 for accommodation (including the mandatory broadcasting licence of €18.36 per month), €200 for food, €80 for laundry, clothing and toiletries, €110 – €150 for health insurance (≈€135 per month for statutory student insurance), €90 for phone, hobbies and sports, and €40 for books and study materials; the semester fee is about €320 each semester (≈€640 per year). Adding a typical transport allowance of €80‑€100 per month brings the monthly total to about €1,080 – €1,300, equating to an annual cost of roughly €12,900 – €15,600. For visa purposes a blocked‑account (Sperrkonto) deposit of about €10,332 is required for the first year, which together with the estimated living expenses results in a total yearly budget of roughly €23,000‑€25,000.

**Yêu cầu học thuật chi tiết**

- International students at the University of Bonn enroll in degree programmes that follow the European Credit Transfer and Accumulation System (ECTS), where a full academic year comprises 60 ECTS (roughly 1 800 hours of combined contact and self‑study) and a semester is normally limited to 30 ECTS (about 18 contact‑hours per week) for exchange participants; bachelor programmes are structured in six semesters with a total of 180 ECTS, of which 168 ECTS are earned through required and elective modules and the remaining 12 ECTS are awarded for the bachelor’s thesis, while each module is defined by explicit learning outcomes that specify the knowledge, skills and competencies to be achieved, and admission to courses generally requires that students have completed the prerequisite modules or possess equivalent prior qualifications, with German‑language programmes demanding proven German proficiency and English‑language programmes requiring adequate English skills, and the credit points assigned to each module reflect the total workload (approximately 25–30 hours per ECTS) covering lectures, seminars, practical work, assignments and examinations.

## **C. XẾP HẠNG, STUDENT VISA, VIỆC LÀM VÀ ĐỜI SỐNG**

**1. Xếp hạng và kiểm định**

- The University of Bonn is a state‑accredited German university whose programmes are recognised by the German Accreditation Council and specialised agencies such as ASIIN, it is placed around 113th globally in the 2024 QS World University Rankings and roughly 125th in the 2024 Times Higher Education World University Rankings, while in QS subject rankings it ranks 55th worldwide (4th in Germany) for Agriculture & Forestry, 57th (1st in Germany) for Mathematics, 61st (2nd in Germany) for Economics & Econometrics and also appears in the global top‑100 for Classics & Ancient History, Biology, Philosophy and Dentistry; with about 31,500 students overall, a substantial proportion are international and the university offers more than 200 academic disciplines, many English‑taught master’s programmes, and active exchange opportunities through Erasmus+ and its Global Exchange Program.

**2. Student visa và chính sách sau tốt nghiệp**

- Visa/student visa: International students admitted to the University of Bonn must show proof of financial resources for their visa, most commonly by opening a blocked account (Sperrkonto) that contains at least €8,640 for the first year (equivalent to the average living cost of €864 per month in Bonn); the blocked account releases a maximum of €992 per month to cover rent, food, health insurance and other expenses, and it is accepted by German authorities for the visa and residence‑permit application. Other acceptable proofs include parental income statements, a German guarantor’s commitment, a bank guarantee or an official scholarship, but the blocked account is the simplest and most widely used method. While studying, non‑EU students are allowed to work in Germany under strict conditions: they may take up to 120 full days or 20 hours per week per calendar year in a part‑time job, which can supplement their budget but generally will not cover all living costs, so the blocked account must still cover the required amount. The Federal Foreign Office and the DAAD website provide detailed guidelines on the financial‑proof requirements and permissible employment for student visa holders. Sau tốt nghiệp: International graduates who have successfully completed a recognised Bachelor’s, Master’s or PhD degree at a German university—including the University of Bonn—are entitled to a post‑study “job‑seeker” residence permit that allows them to stay in Germany for up to 18 months to look for employment that matches their qualification; the 18‑month period starts as soon as the final exam result is issued. To be eligible you must hold a valid passport, proof of graduation (degree certificate or official transcript), proof of sufficient financial resources (e.g., a blocked account showing at least €11 208 per year or a formal declaration of commitment), health‑insurance coverage for the whole period, a current registration certificate (Meldebescheinigung), biometric passport‑size photos and a completed application form submitted to the local Ausländerbehörde. You also need to show evidence of accommodation in Germany and, while not mandatory, a CV, language proficiency proof and any documentation of job‑search activities strengthen the application. The visa fee is about €100, processing typically takes four to six weeks, and once a suitable job is secured you can convert the permit to a regular work visa or EU Blue Card without leaving the country.

**3. Cơ hội việc làm, lương và thị trường**

- Cơ hội việc làm: The University of Bonn supports international students in gaining work experience through its iStart Career Program and the university’s Career Service, which together offer internship placement, Werkstudent (working‑student) positions, company‑tour networking and alumni mentorship; after completing iStart, the proportion of participants who were unemployed fell from 61.4 % at entry to 42.9 % at program end, and among those employed 25.2 % work as student assistants, 12.6 % hold mini‑jobs, 11.8 % have internships or traineeships, 4.2 % volunteer and 3.4 % are in regular employment, illustrating that the program effectively channels students into the German labour market, and overall the university reports that about 90 % of its graduates secure employment or continue in further study within six months, with many moving into research, engineering, finance, consulting and public‑sector careers, often building on Werkstudent or internship experience obtained during their studies. Lương & thị trường: International graduates in Germany, particularly those completing STEM degrees at institutions such as the University of Bonn, enjoy strong labour‑market demand, with engineering accounting for roughly 40 % of all international graduates and over half of international alumni holding degrees in technical or scientific fields; the most sought‑after occupations include mechanical, electrical and automotive engineering, information‑technology and data‑science roles, natural‑science research positions, and finance or consulting jobs that value analytical skills, and starting salaries reflect this demand, typically ranging from about €42 000‑€45 000 for business and natural‑science graduates to €45 000‑€55 000 for engineers and €48 000‑€60 000 for IT specialists, while employment outcomes are high, with roughly 70 % of program participants securing a job within three months after graduation.

**4. Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế và đời sống**

- Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế: The International Office of the University of Bonn serves as the central hub for all international students and doctoral candidates, offering comprehensive counseling on academic, administrative and residency matters, an International Service Point open Monday‑Friday from 08:00 to 16:00 at Poppelsdorfer Allee 53 (phone +49 228 73‑5544, email international@uni‑bonn.de), orientation flyers and a “Bonnews” newsletter, while the Student Registry (phone +49 228 73‑4464, email intstud@verwaltung.uni‑bonn.de) assists prospective students with entry procedures; dedicated staff such as Monique Trachsel (m.trachsel@uni‑bonn.de) and Sandra Papel (s.papel@uni‑bonn.de) coordinate study‑program information and incoming‑exchange support, and a specific housing contact (housing@uni‑bonn.de, phone +49 228 73‑4247) handles accommodation queries, complemented by the Studierendenwerk Bonn which provides student dormitories, dining halls, counseling and financial services; additionally, the university’s Career Service (career@uni‑bonn.de, phone +49 228 73‑7414) offers job‑search workshops, internship listings, CV reviews and employer networking events to help international students transition into the German labour market. Đời sống & môi trường: The University of Bonn provides a lively campus life for international students through several organized groups such as the International Club, which hosts regular cultural evenings and language‑exchange events, the Erasmus Student Network (ESN) that arranges welcome‑day activities, city tours and trips, the “No Worries” group run by the General Students’ Committee and the International Office, various sports teams, and a Study‑Buddy programme that pairs German and overseas students for practical support on housing, insurance and everyday questions; safety on campus is ensured by clearly posted emergency numbers (Police 110, Fire and Ambulance 112), a campus security office, and 24‑hour assistance from the Studentenwerk and the International Office, while Vietnamese students in Bonn have formed their own community—often called Viet‑Studierende Bonn or Vietnamese Student Association— which meets regularly for cultural celebrations, Vietnamese‑language workshops and social outings and is linked to the broader Asian student network and the International Club’s events, making it easy for new Vietnamese arrivals to find friends and reliable information.
