The university offers a single early‑action option, Restrictive Early Action (REA), which is non‑binding and limits applicants from applying early elsewhere; the REA deadline (đầu hạn REA) is November 1 and applicants are notified by mid‑December. The regular‑decision deadline (hạn nộp hồ sơ quyết định thường) is January 1, with decisions released in late March to early April. Required materials include two teacher evaluations, a school‑report transcript, counselor letter, optional SAT/ACT scores, and for REA applicants the mid‑year report is not required at the time of the November deadline (but must be submitted later if deferred). International students follow the same timeline, noting that their academic year may differ, and must ensure their mid‑year grades are sent as soon as available (typically February) if they are deferred to regular decision. - Harvard College admits international applicants who submit an official high‑school transcript (with a certified English translation if the original is not in English) showing courses, grades and the grading scale, and a final school report once grades are posted; while Harvard does not publish a minimum GPA, successful applicants typically have an unweighted GPA of 3.9 / 4.0 or higher and a consistently rigorous curriculum, and the university evaluates the transcript alongside any Advanced Placement, International Baccalaureate, A‑level or comparable national exam results—students may use AP scores, IB diploma results (sent as soon as they are released in mid‑July) or other recognized exams to satisfy the testing requirement if the SAT or ACT cannot be taken, though the SAT or ACT is the standard requirement; for non‑native English speakers an official TOEFL, IELTS or Duolingo English Test score is also required unless the applicant has earned a degree from an English‑language institution; supplementary portfolio materials are optional and may be submitted only for applicants with distinguished talent in the visual or performing arts, and while Harvard does not require an interview, an alumni interview is offered and can provide additional context but is not mandatory for admission.

**3. Tiếng Anh**

- Harvard requires non‑native‑English speakers to demonstrate proficiency with an official TOEFL iBT, IELTS Academic (including the electronic score report), or the Duolingo English Test (DET); the Graduate School of Education also accepts these tests, while Harvard College does not count them toward the mandatory standardized‑testing requirement but allows them to be submitted for review, and the Graduate School of Design lists only TOEFL (no IELTS, DET or PTE) as required. Applicants are exempt from the TOEFL/IELTS/DET requirement if they have earned a bachelor’s degree from an institution where English was the sole language of instruction and they completed at least three full‑time years of study there (a major in English is not sufficient); the Graduate School of Design further specifies that degrees from institutions in Antigua and Barbuda, Australia, the Bahamas, Canada, Ghana, Ireland, Jamaica, New Zealand, Nigeria, Singapore, South Africa, Trinidad and Tobago, the United Kingdom, or the United States automatically satisfy this waiver. Scores must be recent (for the 2025‑2026 cycle, taken on or after 1 January 2023) and sent directly to the appropriate Harvard office; PTE is not listed as an accepted test for any Harvard program.

**Yêu cầu học thuật chi tiết**

- Harvard University requires international students to enroll only in I‑20‑eligible courses that are held on the Harvard campus, meeting session‑specific credit minima—typically eight credits in a seven‑week summer session, four credits in a four‑week session, or a single four‑credit capstone for Extension School degree candidates—while courses listed as online‑only, web‑conference, or pending prerequisites are not eligible for visa purposes; the undergraduate curriculum for all Harvard College students is structured around a total of 128 credits (usually taken as four‑credit courses, 16 credits per regular term), of which at least 84 credits must be earned with a letter grade of C‑ or higher (96 for honors), including mandatory General Education courses in Aesthetics & Culture, Ethics & Civics, Histories, Societies, Individuals, and Science & Technology in Society, as well as writing, foreign‑language, and concentration requirements that can only be satisfied by letter‑graded coursework; most courses follow a standardized syllabus that outlines specific learning outcomes such as mastery of core concepts, development of analytical and communication skills, and the ability to apply knowledge to novel problems, and students must satisfy any listed prerequisites before registration, with holds placed on records until prerequisite completion or advising approval is obtained.

## **C. XẾP HẠNG, STUDENT VISA, VIỆC LÀM VÀ ĐỜI SỐNG**

**1. Xếp hạng và kiểm định**

- Harvard University in the United States enrolls a highly international undergraduate cohort, with international students accounting for about 15 % of the Class of 2029 and a 90 % yield rate among those admitted; the university is consistently placed at the top of major global rankings, holding the #1 position in the 2024 Times Higher Education World University Rankings, the #2 spot in the 2024 QS World University Rankings, and ranking #2 among National Universities in the most recent U.S. News & World Report list, while its subject‑specific performance is equally strong—THE ranks Harvard #1 in Life Sciences, #4 in Physical Sciences, #9 in Computer Science, and #3 in Engineering and Technology, and QS similarly places many of its programs within the top 10 worldwide; academically, Harvard is fully accredited by the New England Commission of Higher Education (NECHE), which serves as its official institutional accrediting body.

**2. Student Visa (F-1) và Chính sách OPT**

- Student Visa (F-1): International students admitted to Harvard University who intend to study on an F‑1 visa must first receive a Form I‑20, which the Harvard International Office (HIO) issues after the student submits proof of sufficient financial resources for at least one academic year; the university requires recent, official bank statements (or equivalent documents such as scholarship award letters, guarantor affidavits, or parent‑sponsored funding letters) that clearly show the amount needed to cover tuition, mandatory fees, and living expenses as calculated by Harvard’s cost‑of‑attendance schedule, and the statements must be on institution‑letterhead, signed, and dated within the past 90 days, with any large deposits explained; once the I‑20 is received, the student must complete the DS‑160 non‑immigrant visa application, pay the SEVIS I‑901 fee (currently $350) using Harvard’s school code BOS214F00162000, and schedule a visa interview at a U.S. embassy or consulate, where U.S. Department of State and USCIS guidance requires the applicant to present the original I‑20, a valid passport, the SEVIS fee receipt, and the same financial documentation submitted to Harvard, demonstrating that the funds are readily available and not contingent on future earnings, to satisfy both the State Department’s visa issuance authority and USCIS’s eligibility criteria for the F‑1 classification. Sau tốt nghiệp (OPT/STEM OPT): The STEM OPT extension gives eligible F‑1 graduates a single 24‑month period of post‑completion work authorization on top of the regular 12‑month OPT, allowing up to 36 months total, and may be used only once per qualifying degree (bachelor’s, master’s or doctorate) that carries a Department of Education CIP code listed on the DHS STEM Designated Degree Program List; to qualify, the student must have a currently approved post‑completion OPT EAD, must file Form I‑765 for the extension up to 90 days before that EAD expires and no later than 60 days after the DSO enters the STEM recommendation in SEVIS, must obtain a new I‑20 with the STEM recommendation, must be employed (or have a job offer) with an employer enrolled in E‑Verify in a position directly related to the field of study, must not exceed a total of 150 days of unemployment across the entire OPT period, and while the properly timed STEM‑OPT petition is pending USCIS automatically authorizes employment for up to 180 days beyond the original OPT expiration, after which the student may continue working only with the approved 24‑month EAD; these rules apply to Harvard University international graduates as they do to all U.S. institutions.

**3. Cơ hội việc làm, lương và thị trường**

- Cơ hội việc làm (CPT/OPT): Harvard’s graduate programs consistently achieve very high post‑graduation employment outcomes, with roughly 90 % of the Class of 2024 securing a job or enrolling in further study within twelve months of graduation and about two‑thirds (67 %) reporting first‑year salaries above $70 k, while employment is distributed across sectors such as higher‑education institutions (27 %), public‑school districts (13 %), direct‑service nonprofits (15 %), academic research (24 %), hospitals/health‑care (43 %) and smaller shares in for‑profit, government, self‑employment and other fields; international students benefit from dedicated support through Harvard’s Mignone Center for Career Success and the Interstride platform, which help them locate and secure internships and co‑op positions that satisfy Curricular Practical Training (CPT) requirements, typically allowing up to twelve months of work authorization and resulting in placement rates that mirror the overall graduate employment figure of about ninety percent. Lương & thị trường: International graduates entering the U.S. labour market earn salaries comparable to their domestic peers and can target occupations that the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics identifies as both high‑paying and rapidly expanding—such as management analysts, which command median annual earnings of $100,000 + and are projected to grow “much faster than average,” financial and investment analysts (median $100,000 +; faster than average growth), computer hardware engineers (median $100,000 +; much faster growth), occupational therapists (median $75,000‑$100,000; much faster growth), genetic counselors (median $75,000‑$100,000; much faster growth), compensation, benefits, and job analysis specialists (median $75,000‑$100,000; faster growth), and materials scientists (median $100,000 +; faster growth). Harvard University’s Job Market Insights tool, which pulls directly from BLS OES data, also reports sector‑specific earnings such as a median $160,139 for marketing managers, illustrating the premium for roles that combine strong demand with advanced skill sets, and these figures can guide international graduates in selecting career paths with both strong salary prospects and robust employment growth.

**4. Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế và đời sống**

- Hỗ trợ sinh viên quốc tế: Harvard’s International Students and Scholars Office (HIO/ISSO) serves all international students and scholars by providing dedicated advisors—such as Reiko Ohmura, Peter O’Meara, Ducis Viard, Monica Chan, Leah Boccaccio, Joseph Chen, Hannah Klare, Kathleen McCarthy, Jenny Minichiello, Martha Gladue, Ivana Hrga‑Griggs, Elizabeth Capuano, Jennifer Havlicek, Jessica Heffernan and Ashlee Anton—who can be reached by email or phone for visa, immigration, and general support. The office runs mandatory HIO orientations for new arrivals that cover essential topics including banking, cell phones, Harvard ID, health insurance, housing options, and safety, and it coordinates with Harvard University Housing to help students secure on‑campus or nearby off‑campus accommodation. For employment, the HIO partners with the Student Employment Office to guide international students through on‑campus job eligibility, Social Security Number requirements, and work‑authorization rules, while the Student Employment Office also offers career‑development resources, job listings, and workshops tailored to international students. Đời sống & môi trường: Harvard University offers a vibrant campus life for international students that includes cultural celebrations such as the annual I‑Night organized by the Harvard Chan Student Association, numerous affinity groups, and a wide array of student organizations—among them the Harvard Vietnamese Student Association, which regularly hosts Tet festivals, language‑exchange meet‑ups and community service projects to connect Vietnamese students and anyone interested in Vietnamese culture; the university also supports Greek life with roughly three dozen fraternities and sororities that welcome eligible international members, while a rich selection of over 400 clubs and extracurriculars—from academic societies to sports teams—provides additional avenues for involvement, and safety is overseen by the Harvard University Police Department, which operates 24/7, offers a campus‑wide emergency notification system, a safety‑alert mobile app, well‑lit pathways, and regular safety workshops, all complemented by the Harvard International Office and the Office for Student Affairs that supply dedicated orientation, visa guidance, housing assistance, and ongoing support services to ensure that international students feel secure and integrated throughout their Harvard experience.
